definition: "Sirach — also known as Ecclesiasticus, the Wisdom of Ben Sira, or the Wisdom of Jesus son of Eleazar son of Sirach — is a Second Temple Jewish wisdom text composed in Hebrew around 180 BCE by a scribe named Joshua ben Sira. Unlike most books in or near the biblical corpus, its authorship and date of composition can be established with reasonable confidence: internal references to the High Priest Simon place composition before 175 BCE, and the author's grandson later translated it into Greek around 116 BCE, explaining his motives in a prologue that stands as one of antiquity's earliest reflections on the practice of translation. Sirach returns on the show primarily because it occupies a uniquely legible position among the texts that competed for biblical status: its authorship is known, its date can be pinned within a 21-year window, its manuscripts survive in Hebrew at Masada and Qumran, and its near-canonical standing is well documented in rabbinic literature. In episode 167's Is It Canon? segment, Dan McClellan walks through what makes Sirach so instructive — the fact that Ben Sira was transparent about being a contemporary author almost certainly cost him canonical inclusion, since a composition date after the prophetic era was disqualifying under the criteria that gradually took hold. The book becomes a case study in how canon formation worked not by theology but by contingent historical forces.

Sirach also surfaces in earlier episodes whenever the show examines the outer edges of the Hebrew Bible. In episode 20's extended discussion of apocryphal and deuterocanonical texts, Dan McClellan highlights that Ecclesiasticus contains one of the earliest known allusions to the concept of a canon — making it both a product of Second Temple Jewish literary culture and a window into the very process by which Jewish scripture was being defined.

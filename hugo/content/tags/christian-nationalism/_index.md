Christian nationalism on the show is less a theology than a power project. The discussions collected here describe it as a cultural framework that merges Christian identity, national identity, and political dominance, then treats that fusion as natural, biblical, and threatened. Episodes repeatedly connect it to fear of pluralism, to racial and gender hierarchy, and to the conviction that preserving a Christian America justifies coercive control over public life.

It also matters because the topic exposes how biblical language gets repurposed in service of modern politics. Guests and hosts return to the way vague proof texts, historical mythmaking, and selective moral outrage can sanctify a grab for power while presenting it as faithfulness. Across these conversations, Christian nationalism becomes a case study in how religion can be used to police belonging, deny equal access to democracy, and recast domination as virtue.

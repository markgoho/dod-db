The Shroud of Turin sits at the intersection of relic devotion, historical argument, and modern pseudoscientific certainty. Episodes on this topic return to how easily weak claims can be made to sound decisive when audiences want material proof for theological commitments, especially when ambiguous tests or sensational headlines are presented as if they settle the question.

It also becomes a recurring example of the difference between dogma and critical inquiry. Discussions collected here focus on provenance, dating methods, medieval artistic context, burial customs, and the way apologetic arguments often treat suggestive evidence as conclusive proof, even when the underlying data point in a different direction.
